Introduction

Motivation

I chose to conduct this Final Project on the impact of gender. I am a woman in STEM who has always been outnumbered in the technical classes that I have taken. I think it is essential for all people - regardless of their gender, race, nor any other distinguishing factors to be treated as equal. One of the largest comments I have always faced is that my gender does has not made a difference - and the struggles I have seen in my journey so far and what I have been able to overcome are not real. I wanted to dedicate this project to all underrepresented people who have felt their journeys have been undermined.

Question

Hence, the focus on my project is exploring the question:
Does gender still play a role in achieving and navigating educational, and professional opportunities?

Overview

I wanted to explore multiple sides to this question which means focusing on the educational disparities in gender, professional disparities, and also tackling the question of time with the usage of the word still in my research question. Preliminary Data Visualization #3 - Unpaid Care Work Female-Male Ratio

Now let’s analyze how much care work men/women do on average to understand where they may be spending their time other than work. This is to see how much women/men work in terms of unpaid labor. I seek to answer the sub-question: How are men/women involved with out of work activities like care without pay?

I found this dataset to work through this: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/imtkaggleteam/womens-right

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

file_path = 'unpaid_work.csv' 
data = pd.read_csv(file_path)

#Sort data by female-male ratio of time devoted to unpaid care work
sorted_data = data.sort_values(by='Female to male ratio of time devoted to unpaid care work (OECD (2014))', ascending=False)

#Select and extract ratios & 15 country names for highest female-male ratios
top_countries = sorted_data.head(15)
top_ratios = top_countries['Female to male ratio of time devoted to unpaid care work (OECD (2014))']
top_entities = top_countries['Entity']

# Create pie chart to visualize distribution of ratios among top 15 countries
plt.figure(figsize=(12, 8))
plt.pie(top_ratios, labels=top_entities, autopct='%1.1f%%', startangle=140)
plt.title('Top 15 Countries by Female to Male Ratio of Unpaid Care Work (2014)\n\n')
plt.axis('equal')
plt.show()

This chart depicts the female to male ratio of unpaid care work in the top 15 countries for 2014. While dated, it reveals a global gender imbalance in the distribution of unpaid labor. In countries like Mali, women contribute 17.3% of the unpaid care work, which alludes to a large burden on women with far-reaching implications. These high percentages suggest that women in the top 15 regions are dedicating a massive portion of their time to caregiving responsibilities, which can limit their participation in paid employment, educational pursuits, and other personal development opportunities. Contrastingly, men who do not have to dedicate their time towards this, can explore these opportunities and continue to grow. Further, this gender disparity in unpaid care work perpetuates a cycle of economic dependency and limits women’s financial independence. In countries like Pakistan (10.2%) and India (9.8%), the heavy load of unpaid care work on women reflects deep-rooted cultural norms and systemic barriers that reinforce traditional gender roles. Women here can find it challenging to pursue careers or engage in economic ventures furthering the gap that exists. Even in countries with lower percentages like Portugal (4.2%), and Cambodia (4.0%), women are always performing more unpaid care work. This imbalance is clearly visible across the world, and addressing it is essential to empower women to be able to fully participate in the economic sector of society rather than being restricted by other duties that men do not share.

Let’s examine in detail how safe and comfortable both men and women feel in the workplace.

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

file_path = 'employee_survey_data.csv'
df = pd.read_csv(file_path)

#Select columns that choose gender and the keyword 'safe and comfortable'
gender_columns = [col for col in df.columns if 'gender' in col.lower()]
comfort_columns = [col for col in df.columns if 'safe and comfortable' in col.lower()]

#If more than one, select the first one
gender_column = gender_columns[0] if gender_columns else None
comfort_column = comfort_columns[0] if comfort_columns else None

#Remove any rows that have missing values in either column
filtered_df = df.dropna(subset=[gender_column, comfort_column])

#Extract gender values and only take male and female 
unique_genders = filtered_df[gender_column].unique()[:2]
gender_mapping = {unique_genders[0]: 'Female', unique_genders[1]: 'Male'}
filtered_df['Gender Mapped'] = filtered_df[gender_column].map(gender_mapping)

#Create a boxplot to visualize distribution of comfort and safety scores by gender
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
sns.boxplot(data=filtered_df, x='Gender Mapped', y=comfort_column, hue='Gender Mapped', palette='viridis')
plt.title('Distribution of Comfort and Safety Felt at Work by Gender')
plt.xlabel('Gender')
plt.ylabel('Comfort and Safety Score')
plt.xticks(rotation=45)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

This box plot allows us to gain a comprehensive understanding of what exactly is felt by each of the genders in the workplace. Additionally, we can understand the spread of female / male emotions rather than examining an average to truly capture all the data including outliers. The key findings show that females on average feel less comfortable and safe at work compared to their male colleagues. The median comfort and safety score for Women is 4 while Males is 5. Hence, on average it can be determined that men have a more comfortable experience in the work environment. The IQR range (middle 50% of scores) is slightly broader for Males indicating more variance in experiences of safety versus discomfort. For females, this range is more concentrated around the lower median which highlights a constant issue with workplace comfort and safety. Examining outliers can also show that there are some individuals with unusually low scores - likely due to harassment, explicit discrimination, or other adverse circumstances - but also some individuals with high scores for both Men and Women. Generally, the observed disparity in feelings of safety and comfort can lead to lower job satisfaction, higher stress, and decreased productivity in women, and can explain lower career progression/retention rates. They may choose to leave such jobs or avoid some industries altogether.

The main question to tackle now remains: Does gender still play a role in achieving and navigating educational, and professional opportunities?

The question of whether gender still plays a role in educational and professional opportunities is complex. While significant progress towards gender equality in recent decades has been made, the data I discussed reveals how gender continues to be a key factor in shaping experiences and outcomes across Professional and Educational pursuits.

From an educational standpoint, the encouraging findings from the data include the narrowing gap in educational attainment between men and women. Women are achieving higher levels of education at rates comparable to or even surpassing men in many ways. The data indicates that there is only a 2.5% gap between males and females in advanced education degrees. Hence, gender doesn’t necessarily hinder progress in education. This suggests that access to education and the ability to attain academic qualifications have improved for women, reflecting positive societal changes in attitudes towards women’s education. But, this increased educational attainment doesn’t necessarily translate into professional opportunities. Despite their higher levels of academic success, women often face structural barriers that limit their career advancement. This disconnect between educational achievement and professional progress underscores the ongoing impact of gender on career progression, pointing to the presence of systemic issues like racial biases, and unequal opportunities in the workforce.

In terms of professional progress, the data on occupational segregation clearly shows how gender shapes it. Certain fields remain heavily male-dominated, particularly those involving technical expertise and high-stakes decision-making roles. This indicates gender imbalance, showing historical trends where men occupy roles that are perceived as requiring greater skill or responsibility. On the other hand, women are predominantly found in roles characterized as supportive or lower-wage positions, such as secretarial work, cleaning, and assistant roles. The data also reveals that women disproportionately shoulder the burden of unpaid care responsibilities across the globe, which hinders their ability to engage in professional endeavors. In terms of comfort and safety, women, on average, report feeling less comfortable and safe at work compared to their male counterparts. In terms of Leadership, despite a slight upward trend, women remain significantly underrepresented in leadership roles such as CEOs and senators. Finally, the significant wage gap is also evidence of the gender inequality in professional opportunities, and undervaluation of women’s contributions.

In essence, it is crucial to make progress to ensure that women are respected and valued for their contributions in both professional and educational settings.
